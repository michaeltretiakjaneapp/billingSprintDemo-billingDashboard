Claim Creation

How a claim gets built after an appointment — from arriving the visit through adding billing codes, ICD-10 diagnoses, modifiers, and confirming the rendering provider, to the claim landing in the right folder ready to submit. Covers both the chart-first and appointment-panel-first paths. Signal from #pt-billing-service, #support_usa_billing, #pt-insurance-foundations, #proj-insurance-ai, and #jane_community_posts_fb.

G1 Claim lands in Unsubmitted with the correct CPT codes, ICD-10s, modifiers, and rendering provider — no missing fields
G2 Biller knows the claim is ready to submit without having to check the chart status manually
G3 Recurring visits don't require re-entering codes from scratch every time
0
Arrive the Appointment
Step 0.1
Admin or practitioner marks appointment as Arrived
Clinic Admin
Task
Arrive the appointment to generate the invoice and claim draft
▶
Suggested Opportunity
Arriving an appointment is the trigger that creates the claim — but there's no visual "billing checklist" shown at arrival to prompt the admin to confirm insurance is attached, billing codes are set, and the chart is assigned to the right practitioner before the visit starts.
Jane System
Task
Generate invoice and place claim in Draft folder
▶
Pain Point
Claims land in Draft with no CPT or ICD-10 codes if default billing codes aren't set up — billers find $0 "no charge" claims sitting in Unsubmitted with no indication of what's missing or why
Community"The first two unsubmitted claims look like they don't have any billing codes, which is why they were no charge and not submitted." — Karyna, #hot_seat_support
Suggested Opportunity
Surface a "no billing codes" warning on Draft claims in the Unsubmitted folder — with a direct link to add codes — so billers can action them without clicking into each claim individually.
1
Add Billing Codes (CPT / Service Codes)
Step 1.1
Path A: Default codes apply automatically from session type or policy
Jane System
Task
Apply pre-configured default billing codes to the claim
▶
Opportunity
Recurring appointments now carry forward CPT codes, ICD-10s, and modifiers from the source visit — recently shipped. For clinics with consistent recurring billing, this eliminates the most common re-entry burden.
Feedback"Recurring appointments now behave much more like copying an appointment. When a user recurs an insured appointment, we'll carry forward all the codes present on the source visit." — Mosh, #features
Pain Point
Clinics that don't discover default billing codes end up manually adding CPT codes to every single appointment — a daily burden that becomes one of the biggest sources of early billing frustration and missed codes
Community"Staff are manually verifying CPT codes and insurance policies for each session, leading to time-consuming and error-prone workflows." — Kelsey, #win_loss_insights
Step 1.2
Path B: Practitioner codes from the chart using #hashtag syntax
Practitioner
Task
Type CPT and ICD-10 codes into the chart using #code hashtag syntax
▶
Pain Point
Typing out every code as a hashtag (#98941 #99204 #m54.50) for every visit is slow and error-prone — practitioners find this workflow burdensome and it's a recurring pain point in the Facebook community
Community"For clinics that code via the chart, typing out every single code can feel very time consuming. Users are looking for quick ways to document these codes in charting flows that actually allow them to push to the appointment panel/claim." — Mosh, #pt-insurance-foundations
Opportunity
Auto-suggested diagnosis codes based on the chart's clinical content ("diagnose first, then apply treatment") was raised as a near-ideal workflow by billers — pulling ICD-10s from the chart narrative rather than requiring manual hashtag entry.
Community"In a perfect world, she would love for the diagnoses codes to be auto-suggested in the chart entry. Then, pulled into the next step." — Jeremy, via Stefanie (biller), #proj-insurance-ai
Practitioner
Task
Sign the chart and push codes to the appointment / claim
▶
Pain Point
If codes are pushed from the chart to a claim that was already submitted, Jane marks the claim as Unsubmitted again — creating a second submission that billers must catch and de-duplicate
Community"If the notes are being done after the claim is submitted, if they're updating the codes from that chart, the practitioner can check to see if that claim has been submitted already." — Mosh, #support_usa_clearinghouses
Suggested Opportunity
When codes are pushed from a chart to an already-submitted claim, surface a clear prompt: "This claim has already been submitted — do you want to create a corrected claim, or mark this as reviewed?" Rather than silently moving the claim back to Unsubmitted.
Step 1.3
Path C: Biller manually adds codes via appointment panel or Unsubmitted folder
Biller
Task
Search for and add CPT codes to the claim via "Edit Billing Codes"
▶
Pain Point
The fee attached to each billing code is not visible in the Claim Submissions list view — billers must click into each claim individually to verify the code has the correct charge amount, adding significant time on large batch reviews
Community"Within the Claim Submissions view, it's cumbersome to view the fee attached to a billing code. Especially in cases where there are No Charge invoices as a clinic may have added an improper Billing Code. It requires clicking the View button, then viewing the Billing Code to ensure the correct one is attached." — Ethan, #pt-billing-service
Opportunity
Showing the dollar amount and billing code inline in the Unsubmitted list column would let billers scan for $0 or incorrect-fee claims at a glance — without clicking into each one.
2
Add Diagnosis Codes & Modifiers
Step 2.1
Biller or practitioner adds ICD-10 diagnosis codes
Biller
Task
Select ICD-10 codes and verify they are in the correct order for the claim
▶
Pain Point
ICD-10 code order matters — the primary diagnosis must be listed first. Rearranging diagnosis codes generates a new invoice version, creating multiple invoice records for the same visit and confusion in reporting
Community"Rearranged the order of their ICD-10 codes at 4:37pm — that's the fourth and fifth patient invoice for the same visit." — Mosh, documenting a multi-invoice scenario, #pt-insurance-foundations
Pain Point
There is no Jane-enforced guidance on which ICD-10s are valid with which CPT codes — Jane relies on the user to select appropriate combinations, and coding errors (wrong code sets, mismatched diagnoses) only surface as clearinghouse rejections after submission
Community"Jane does not store or enforce any ICD-10 ↔ CPT linkage in the codebase today. We rely on the user to select appropriate and defensible combinations." — John Whaley, #pt-insurance
Opportunity
Advisory (non-enforced) CPT ↔ ICD-10 pairing suggestions — based on historical usage patterns within Jane's data — would help billers select valid combinations without introducing hard restrictions. John Whaley noted this is where Roux rules could be meaningful.
Step 2.2
Biller checks and applies required modifiers
Biller
Task
Apply the correct modifier(s) for the payer, discipline, and service type
▶
Pain Point
Modifier rules are complex — which modifier to apply varies by payer, discipline, state, and even whether care is acute vs maintenance. There is no in-Jane guidance to tell a biller whether a modifier is required, optional, or prohibited for a given combination
Community"Some insurers or disciplines require the use of these modifiers more often than others. It can be challenging to keep track of when to use them and when not to use them." — Mosh, #proj-insurance-ai
Opportunity
Modifier rules as configurable Roux-style rules — e.g. "flag any Medicare claim with CPT 98941 that's missing the AT modifier" — would surface missing or incorrect modifiers before submission, not after rejection.
Biller
Task
Verify billing code / diagnosis / modifier combinations are correct for the insurer
▶
Pain Point
Having some type of database to automate known billing code / diagnosis code / modifier combinations for specific modalities has been directly requested by billers — e.g. if A and B are present on a policy we need modifier C, if A is by itself we do not
Community"Having some type of database to reference and ideally automate if known billing code/diagnosis code combinations are present for specific modalities. If they are, we add modifiers based on a set of rules." — Ethan, #pt-billing-service
Opportunity
Rule-based modifier automation (Roux) is the clearest opportunity here — letting billers define rules like "if CPT 98941 + Medicare + no AT modifier → flag" would catch the most common modifier errors across the entire claim queue.
3
Confirm Rendering Provider
Step 3.1
Biller verifies the rendering provider on the claim is correct
Biller
Task
Confirm the rendering provider NPI and billing provider NPI are set correctly for this insurer
▶
Pain Point
When both billing provider NPI and rendering provider NPI resolve to the same value, some insurers (e.g. Availity/Highmark) reject the claim outright — this mismatch is invisible in Jane until the clearinghouse sends back a rejection
Community"Billing provider identifier loop 2010AA NM109 cannot be the same as the rendering provider identifier loop 2310B NM109." — Real clearinghouse rejection, #support_usa_billing
Suggested Opportunity
A pre-submission check that flags when the billing and rendering provider NPIs resolve to the same value would catch this common rejection before the 837 is transmitted.
Biller
Task
Confirm chart author matches the intended rendering provider on the claim
▶
Pain Point
If the chart author changes after a claim is submitted (e.g. a supervisor corrects a supervisee's note), the claim may show a different rendering provider than who actually treated the patient — Jane does not automatically flag this discrepancy
Community"The claim could have included the 'wrong' rendering provider name and credentials. If the insurer ever audits the clinic, the documentation supporting that date of service could be under a different practitioner than what the insurer has on file." — John Whaley, #incidents
4
Claim Moves to "Ready to Submit"
Step 4.1
Biller confirms claim is in Unsubmitted folder with all codes complete
Biller
Task
Check Unsubmitted folder and verify claim status, chart status, and code completeness
▶
Pain Point
There's no reliable signal in the Unsubmitted folder telling the biller whether a claim is truly ready to submit — chart status is not shown, missing codes are not flagged, and a $0 claim with no codes looks identical to a correctly priced one at a glance
Community"One thing the team has been looking at is better visibility in the claim submission folders to know when a claim is actually ready to be submitted — specifically, the chart status for those entries made visible when perusing the Unsubmitted folders." — Mosh, #support_usa_clearinghouses
Opportunity
Surfacing chart status (Draft / Signed / None) directly in the Unsubmitted folder column view would let billers instantly separate "ready to submit" claims from "waiting on the practitioner to sign their chart" — eliminating the most common cause of premature claim submission.
Biller
Task
Check claim status indicator on the schedule — $0 balance doesn't mean claim is submitted
▶
Pain Point
The paid indicator on the schedule doesn't distinguish between a genuinely paid and submitted claim vs an unsubmitted claim where the balance just happens to be $0 — billers and admins misread the schedule and assume billing is complete when it isn't
Community"The symbol doesn't distinguish between a patient who's genuinely paid and whose claim is submitted, versus a patient with an unsubmitted claim where the balance just happens to be $0." — Maddie Maerz, #pt-insurance
Suggested Opportunity
A separate "claim submitted" indicator on the schedule appointment card — distinct from "balance $0" — would prevent billers and front desk staff from assuming billing is complete based on a $0 balance alone.
Step 4.2
Biller signals claim is ready — practitioner or billing workflow completes handoff
Biller
Task
Use Draft → Unsubmitted as a practitioner-to-biller handoff signal
▶
Pain Point
Some clinics train practitioners to manually move claims from Draft to Unsubmitted as a signal that everything is ready — but this is a non-obvious workaround that asks practitioners to understand billing folder logic, which often leads to errors
Community"Cortney teaches some of her clients to have providers move their claims from draft to unsubmitted once they've added everything they need to, and the billing team treats that as signal the claim is ready to go. At this clinic's scale though that's tricky, and asking practitioners to do stuff like this often leads to error." — John Whaley, #jane_community_posts_fb
Opportunity
A first-class "ready to submit" flag — set by the practitioner from within the chart or appointment panel — would replace the current Draft/Unsubmitted folder workaround with an explicit, intuitive signal that doesn't require practitioners to understand billing folder mechanics.
